Intro

Hi, my name is Giovanni Gollotti, but you can call me Gio! I am currently a second-year Astrophysics Master's student at the City University of New York (CUNY) Graduate Center. In 2025, I graduated with a Bachelor's of Science in Physics & Astronomy from the University of Washington.

As a sophomore, I first researched template generation strategies for the commissioning phase and first year of operations for the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). For a brief time during junior year, I visually inspected quasar spectra from Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS-V) to find changing-look quasars, which visually change on human timescales (months to years). My most impactful research occurred during my senior year, where I analyzed the photometric and radial velocity variabilities of the young, active M0 dwarf HIP 23309. Post-graduation, I worked as a remote post-baccaleurate research assistant for Northwestern University. I created globular cluster cutouts of "brick" images from the Panchromatic Hubble Andromeda Treasury (PHAT) Stellar Cluster Survey, taken with the Hubble Space Telescope (HST).

Currently I am searching for nitrogen-rich M dwarfs in the nearby field within the SDSS-V Baryonic Oscillation Spectroscopic Survey (BOSS) database. Nitrogen is important for studying how the Milky Way's stellar halo builds over time, since it is an important tracer for determining whether a star originated from a globular cluster. No one has ever identified a nitrogen-rich M dwarf before, so my research is brand new! For more information on my project, please refer to the Research tab of my website. I hope to continue using M dwarfs as "fossil records" for mapping the evolution of the Milky Way over time, since M dwarfs essentially live forever and maintain the same chemical composition throughout their whole life (as long as they're not in binaries).

Notable Research

Data-Driven Identification of Candidate Nitrogen-Rich M Dwarfs

For my Master's thesis, I am working with Dr. Carrie Filion to identify candidate nitrogen-rich M dwarfs in the nearby field, using both observations from the SDSS-V Baryonic Oscillation Spectropscopic Survey (BOSS) database and synthetic spectral grids between 3500 - 4100 K. I am trying to identify enriched M dwarfs that could have escaped from a globular cluster (GC), since ejected stars from GCs build up the Milky Way's stellar halo over time. GCs have two distinct stellar populations: one with typical chemical compositions similar to field stars, and another enriched in nitrogen (N), aluminum (Al), helium (He), and sodium (Na) / depleted in carbon (C), oxygen (O), and magnesium (Mg). To date, no one has ever discovered a nitrogen-rich M dwarf, and there is no literature on how exactly nitrogen affects an M dwarf spectrum.

Since we currently do not know which spectral features are associated with nitrogen, we generated synthetic spectral grids with constant temperature and varying Fe, C, N, and O abundances. That way, we'll know exactly how much of a given element is present within the modeled stellar atmospheres. To identify which elements are represented by specific spectral features, I broke the spectral grid into its 8 basic components using the Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) method. Each component represents a unique spectral feature. In simpler terms, I'm seeing which features vary the most per spectrum. Using representative abundances from the M107 globular cluster populations, nitrogen enrichment appears to decrease the flux between 5000 - 7000 Angstroms.

I'm currently creating heat maps to see which spectral features are associated with nitrogen, so I'll add more updates soon!

Two Intriguing Transits of HIP 23309 Observed by TESS

During my fourth year as an undergraduate, I studied the photometric and radial velocity variabilities of the young, active M0 dwarf HIP 23309 under the supervision of two previous Ph.D. students, Dr. Andy Tzanidakis and Dr. Tobin Wainer, and Prof. James R.A. Davenport. I analyzed ~5 years of light curves taken with the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), and luckily HIP 23309 is located in its southern continuous viewing zone. I found two dips in the light curves: a small one during 2021 (Sector 35) and a larger asymmetrical one during 2023 (Sector 62). At the time, I only found two dips, even though I had over 5 years worth of nearly continuous data. In 2026, I detected two more dips (Sectors 34 and 68 respectively) in the TESS light curves, which were both asymmetrical in nature.

Along with the photometric TESS data, I also analyzed radial velocity (RV) data taken from 2004-2006 and 2014-2015 with the High Accuracy Radial velocity Planet Searcher (HARPS) Spectrograph at La Silla Observatory (courtesy of Trifonov et. al (2021)). Radial velocity measurements reveal whether a star is experiencing stellar magnetic activity (starspots & flares), being diminished by dust, or being gravitationally impacted by a companion (either a star, brown dwarf, or planet). With some quick blackbody models and SED fits, we determined the dips were not caused by dust. In order to differentiate between stellar magnetic activity ("stellar jitter") and companion transits, we used a simple sinusoid as our possible orbital model. After plotting 100 random orbital models with a Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC), we couldn't rule out stellar jitter as a possibility, since our orbital models weren't fully constrained due the our sparse RV data. If the dips aren't caused by stellar jitter, another possibility could be that they represent transits of a brown dwarf with a circumstellar ring.

In October and December 2025, I obtained 14 additional radial velocity measurements from the CTIO-1.5m CHIRON spectrograph at Cerro Tololo Observatory. I ran a rejection sampling method on both the CHIRON and HARPS RV measurements bu unfortunately didn't find any conclusive trend due to HIP 23309's prominent stellar magnetic activity. Even with the new data, I still have no idea what is causing HIP 23309's dips to occur. If you have any insight or ideas, please contact me, because I'm always open to new collaborations. I hope to continue constraining the stellar magnetic activity of young, active M dwarfs such as HIP 23309, so I can better understand this unique behavior.

About Me
