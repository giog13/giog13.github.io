Intro

Hi, my name is Giovanni Gollotti, but you can call me Gio! I am currently working towards my Astrophysics Master's degree at the City University of New York (CUNY) Graduate Center and will graduate in 2027. In 2025, I graduated with a Bachelor's of Science in Physics & Astronomy from the University of Washington, where I was deeply involved with the astronomy department.

As a sophomore, I first researched template generation strategies for the commissioning phase and first year of operations for the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). For a brief time during junior year, I visually inspected quasar spectra from Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS-V) to find changing-look quasars, which visually change on human timescales (months to years). My most impactful research occurred during my senior year, where I analyzed the photometric and radial velocity variabilities of the young, active M0 dwarf HIP 23309. I fell in love with low-mass stars and time-domain astrophysics as a whole. Post-graduation, I worked as a remote post-baccaleurate research assistant at Northwestern University, where I created cutout images of "brick" images from the Panchromatic Hubble Andromeda Treasury (PHAT) Stellar Cluster Survey taken with the Hubble Space Telescope (HST).

Now I am attending CUNY for graduate school, and I have yet to join a new research group. If you have any projects regarding observational stellar astrophysics, please contact me!

Notable Research

Two Intriguing Transits of HIP 23309 Observed by TESS

During my fourth year as an undergraduate, I studied the photometric and radial velocity variabilities of the young, active M0 dwarf HIP 23309 under the supervision of Prof. James R.A. Davenport and two astronomy Ph.D candidates, Andy Tzanidakis and Tobin Wainer. I analyzed ~5 years of light curves taken with the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), since HIP 23039 is located in the southern continuous viewing zone. In the light curve data, I found two dips: a small one during 2021 (Sector 35) and a larger asymmetrical one during 2023 (Sector 62). I only found two dips, even though I had over 5 years worth of nearly continuous data. "What caused these dips to occur?" is the question my research group and I wanted to answer.

Along with the photometric TESS data, I also analyzed radial velocity (RV) data taken from 2004-2006 and 2014-2015 with the High Accuracy Radial velocity Planet Searcher (HARPS) Spectrograph at La Silla Observatory (courtesy of Trifonov et. al (2021)). Radial velocity measurements reveal whether a star is experiencing stellar magnetic activity (starspots & flares), being diminished by dust, or being gravitationally impacted by a companion (either a star, brown dwarf, or planet). With some quick blackbody models and SED fits, we determined the dips were not caused by dust. In order to differentiate between stellar magnetic activity ("stellar jitter") and companion transits, we used a simple sinusoidal as our possible orbital model. After plotting 100 random orbital models with a Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC), we can't rule out stellar jitter as a possibility, since our orbital models still aren't constrained with the limited RV data we currently have. If the dips aren't caused by stellar jitter, another possibility is that they represent transits of a brown dwarf with a circumstellar ring.

We will soon obtain new RV data later this year. This project is still a work in progress, so check back here for future updates.
